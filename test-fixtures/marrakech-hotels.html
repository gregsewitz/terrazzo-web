Our Marrakech Hotels List

by

Team Yolo
Mar 13, 2026

1 Like(s)

Marrakech has always drawn waves of visitors looking for something a little freer, more sensory—from the bohemian expats of the 1960s and ‘70s (think Yves Saint Laurent, Talitha Getty and their ilk), to today’s designers, artists, and stylish long-weekenders. In the last five years, the hotel scene has grown significantly, with a proliferation of small, charming riads opening in the medina and personality-filled countryside retreats beyond the city walls.

The first decision for any visitor is whether you want to stay inside the medina’s maze of alleys, or outside it. And if outside, just how far? Some of us love waking up steps from the hurlyburly of Jemaa el-Fnaa and the souks; others prefer dipping into the old city and then retreating to palm groves, olive farms, horse stables, or Atlas Mountain views, all within a relatively easy drive. (Or, combine the two for a city-country stay!)  

Our guide gathers places we’ve either stayed at or come recommended, all within roughly an hour of the historic center, moving clockwise around the city. For what to do once you’re there—from lunch in a 1930s pétanque club to a tagine dinner in opulent splendor; visiting the best family-owned rug shop, a museum with a stunning jewelry collection, or most spoiling hammam—have a look at our Marrakech Black Book. 

The Medina/Historic Center

The beating heart of Marrakech, the medina is a UNESCO-listed labyrinth of narrow streets, souks, and centuries of history, anchored by Jemaa el-Fnaa square, where snake charmers, musicians and food stalls take over at night. Just minutes from the sensory overload, true calm lies behind several unassuming doors that open into charming boutique riads with jewelbox pools, shady gardens and expansive roof terraces.

Dar Kawa – Owned by Valerie Barkowski—of the eponymous linens workshop/boutique—this minimalist 5-room riad is “a deep-dive into the possibilities of a black-and-white palette,” according to a stylist friend who loved it, with vernacular four-poster beds strewn with Amazigh rugs and stylish seating areas arranged around the courtyard garden. Also: the chef makes an amazing homemade couscous.  

Izza Marrakech – If a nostalgia for the bohemian spirit of 1970s Marrakech rings your bell, you’ll feel at home in Izza’s decadent interiors—homages to American interior designer Bill Willis, who defined the lavish Moroccan-modernist style of the era. The 14-room warren of interlinked riads is best known for its $6.5 million art collection, an unrestrained mix of Moroccan contemporary art and international photography (plus an artist residency that supports local talent). 

L’Hotel Marrakech – The first of British designer Jasper Conran’s two Morocco hotels (the other is YSL’s former home, Villa Mabrouka, in Tangier), the riad is small but impactful, with the chicest striped upholstery, lacquered doors, and the designer’s own antiques and art collection elegantly placed around a property that never feels overstuffed. 

La Mamounia – The most legendary hotel in town, attracting the glitterati and bucket listers since 1923. The lobby feels a bit theatrical with international brands like Dior, Pierre Hermé and Jean Georges, skewing more Vegas than old-world Morocco. But Yolanda loved the long, moody hallways lined with black-and-white vintage photography, the spacious rooms with balconies, 20 acres of lush gardens, and especially the intimate Churchill bar, where Mr. Hafid makes perfect Tanqueray 10 martinis. (Read her Guest Book here.) 

Le Farnatchi – Owned and run by hotelier James Wix (cofounder of travel designer/Yolo Navigator Wix Squared), the riad’s 9 rooms, some with fireplaces and balconies, feel traditionally Moroccan, with ornate ceilings and intricately crafted detail. Ideal for a group takeover, there’s a separate full-service spa just next door.

P’tit Habibi – Five rooms tucked deep in the medina near the spice market, mixing traditional materials with modern details that feel trendy but not overbearing. The Norwegian architect-owner has filled the house with art, books and other personal objects that feel coded to younger travelers. 

Riad 42 – More Rick Owens than Moroccan maximalism, with everything reduced to its essentials: cool tadelakt walls and floors, heavy woolen blankets, a monochrome palette. Owned by a French-Belgian couple, it’s just 3 guest rooms and 2 suites around a bright inner courtyard flanked by banana trees. Starting at $112/night, the price is right. 

Riad Brummell – Little sister to Maison Brummell (below), this 19th-century palace embodies the thoughtful vision of Moroccan artist Rachid Bargamane in a more intimate package. Terracotta lime-washed walls glow warmly, multiple fountains splash peacefully, the 5 rooms are generously proportioned, and the sweeping rooftop terrace is the place to be come evening. 

Riad Dar Rbaa Laroub – One of the first to open in the medina over 40 years ago, Jean Noël Schoeffer’s soulful guesthouse has remained a beacon of bohemian, collected living ever since (and muse to YSL Museum designers Studio KO, who lived there when they arrived in the city). The seven guest rooms are an effortlessly cool mix of antiques, art, books and other personal touches, creating an “intimate and nostalgic ambience and warm hospitality,” according to an artist friend who knows it well. At just €75 a night, we can live without AC or a pool.

Riad El Fenn – This Instagram darling was a game changer when it opened two decades ago; it now embodies 21 boldly decorated rooms across 12 buildings, three pools, an always-hopping rooftop bar with its iconic red-and-white striped umbrellas, three swimming pools, and a must-visit boutique with a jewelry collection by Marisa Berenson. Founded by expats, the hotel just came under fifth-gen Marrakeshi ownership, who are keeping the iconic property’s commitment to the arts with the addition of a mural by Moroccan artist Rita Alaoui.

Riad Jardin Secret –  The hideaway’s name was inspired by its clandestine past: two identical riads constructed next to each other by a rich Marrakchi for his two lovers. Now it’s a lovely guest house with beautiful carved and painted ceilings, thanks to the preservationist instincts of its new owners, Parisian fashion veterans turned gallerists. 

Riad Kniza – Marrakech antiques dealer and former tour guide, Mohamed Bouskri, revived the mansion that’s been in his family for 200 years with a crack team of traditional artisans—think carved wooden doors, elaborate tilework and hand-woven textiles. The rooms are maximalist and quite heavy/traditional, but the transporting pool area and Moroccan restaurant are draws. 

Riad Mena – The 7-room retreat behind an unmarked door is our favorite place to stay in Marrakech, not just for its refreshingly minimalist character in a city of sensory overload—a palm-shaded oasis with an impeccable mix of vintage midcentury pieces, Moroccan-crafted decor and contemporary art. But because its British owner, Philomena Schurer Merckoll, and her familial staff have cultivated a clubby atmosphere that makes you feel like you’ve scored a coveted invite to someone’s private house party. Fortunately for us, she just opened Dar Mena, a 4-bedroom annex next door! (Read our Guest Book here.) 

Riad No. 37 – We’re into the grey, tonal design vibe of this riad, which leans more austere than most, with thoughtfully placed craft touches. Well set-up for a full takeover (8 rooms), they also offer artist residencies.  

Riad Romeo – Italian fashion designer Romeo Gigli and his wife, Lara, turned their family home into a five-room guest house during the pandemic, and it’s a portal into his playful world from the moment you enter, with a canary-yellow tiled fountain resembling an abstract smiley face. Inside the vibe continues with juicy-hued tiles, painted headboards, and custom crafted furniture and lighting. The riad is run by their daughter, Diletta, and when our deputy editor Alex stayed, Lara herself stepped in to whip up a bowl of spaghetti pomodoro, completing the feeling of living in a family home.

Riad Tarabel – The French owner’s aristocratic upbringing can be felt across the spacious rooms here, with design cues that mix classic Moroccan with French antiques (Provencal furniture, rattan), family heirlooms, and a lovely pool on an expansive lawn. 

Rosemary – Laurence Leenaert, the Belgian designer behind beloved homeware brand Lrnce, opened this intimate 5-bedroom riad in the heart of the medina in 2023. It’s a showcase for local craftsmanship in a soothing palette of dusky pinks and golds; textured walls, terrazzo and zellige; and featuring the studio’s own playfully geometric textiles and ceramics—the rooftop patio is one of our favorite spots for an aperitivo and the little hammam looks amazing.  

Royal Mansour – It’s the Moroccan King’s own hotel—and feels like it. The property was built as a kind of private medina of luxury, where guests stay in 53 individual three-story riads, each with its own courtyard, rooftop terrace and plunge pool. More than 1,000 Moroccan artisans worked on it, filling the interiors with carved cedar, intricate tilework, and hand-plastered walls. The result is grand yet serene, with welcoming staff that feel unscripted—and while definitely a splurge, a friend advises, “you can rent a poolside pavilion with friends and while the day away, have the best hammam in town, and enjoy a delicious poolside lunch on your pavilion terrace.”

The Mellah Hotel – Named for the Jewish Quarter where it sits, this new 10-room riad is the passion project of Moroccan entrepreneur Simohamed Azzouz. Interiors mix zellige tile, vintage and custom-crafted midcentury-style furniture, and paintings by Moroccan artists, including Bouchra Boudoua. The showstopper: a 32-foot-long rooftop pool perfect for sunset cocktails.

Villa Des Orangers – Friends who’ve stayed here for years love it for its location on the edge of the medina, the large guest rooms (33 of them) that incorporate beautiful local craftsmanship, three pools, excellent service and food (it’s Relais & Chateaux), and enchanting evening ambiance “that makes you feel like you’re in a movie.” 

Majorelle/Guéliz (Ville Nouvelle – North)

These adjacent neighborhoods just outside the medina walls form the stylish core of the Ville Nouvelle, the modern city laid out during the French protectorate. Guéliz leans more cosmopolitan—lined with cafés, galleries and boutiques—while Majorelle is more residential, home to artists and creatives outside of the medina, and of course YSL’s famed Jardin Majorelle. 

Dar Rhizlane – A friend calls it “the Mini-Mounia” for its unique combination of exotic Morocco and art-déco French savoir-faire, at an affordable price. He says, “Perfectly located amongst leafy avenues. Beautiful rooms, colorfully tiled bathrooms, Majorelle blue walls, a magical garden, perfect pool, delicious cuisine, and smiles from everyone.” 

Maison Brummell – Around the corner from the YSL Museum and Jardin Majorelle, the location alone is a plus. So are the rooms that were inspired, a friend and fan tells us, by the Austrian owner’s research trip through Geoffrey Bawa’s Sri Lanka, which explains the breezy indoor-outdoor feeling. With breakfast served in the poolside living room with poufs arranged around a low coffee table and a fab in-house boutique, we’re sold. 

The Four Seasons – Most convenient to the airport and Palais des Congrès conference center, it’s ideal for business travelers who don’t want to get swallowed by the chaos of the medina. Expectedly upscale (if somewhat generic) rooms, two pools, a large spa, expansive grounds, and a lovely poolside restaurant. Also a good family option, with a dedicated children’s pool.  

Palmeraie (Northeast)

Just northeast of the city center, the Palmeraie is a calm oasis and a distinct contrast to the intensity of the medina. It’s essentially a palm desert and residential suburb, with properties here boasting more space and outdoor activities—and bigger pools. 

Jnane Rumi – A 12-room garden retreat that feels like an art-filled family home. Founded by Dutch couple Corinne van den Bergh—a somatic therapist and sculptor—and her husband, art lawyer Gert-Jan, it began as a place to gather with family and friends before evolving into an open-door creative oasis. “The family’s art collection offers a playful, intimate point of view, grounded by the work of local artisans,” our friend who stayed for a retreat put it. “Pretense isn’t on the menu.” Speaking of which, she also loved the food. (Read more on her stay here.)

Jnane Tamsna – A nine-acre garden retreat owned by Meryanne Loum‑Martin—recognized as the first Black female hotel owner in Morocco—and her ethnobotanist husband. Interiors blend North and West African design influences with colorful antiques-filled rooms, a leafy pool area and clay tennis court. Alex stayed and loved how involved Loum-Martin is in the life of the house—hosting dinners, guiding guests through gardens, and sharing stories on Moroccan culture and architecture.

Ksar Char-Bagh – Reportedly a favorite of the Moroccan royal family, it’s a sanctuary 20 minutes outside the city designed to just chill—in one of the huge guest room’s sunken bathtubs, by the palm-surrounded pool, or in the gobsmacking red marble hammam. 

Les Deux Tours – One of the larger properties in Palmeraie and owned by the same Moroccan family that recently acquired El Fenn, it manages to balance scale and taste. We like the look of the pool suites, each equipped with a private garden and plunge. Families will appreciate the 3-BR La Tour suites and the property’s large pool. 

Route de Ouarzazate (East/Southeast)

This road takes you out of Marrakech going southeast towards the city of Ouarzazate, through a landscape of olive groves, farms and estates. Thanks to its distance, properties here tend to have spacious grounds and vast gardens, while still being an easy drive back to the center of town.

Amanjena – A classic Aman sanctuary (and splurge) outside the city. Most rooms are standalone pavilions with gardens and sometimes pools, all wrapped in terracotta walls, perfectly pruned gardens, and total serenity.

Farasha Farmhouse – More clubhouse than hotel, event planners Fred and Rosena Charmoy turned an olive farm into a gathering place for local and visiting creatives, with long communal lunches and dinners on big wooden tables. Guests linger by the pool all day and explore the regenerative farm (with chickens and donkeys!), and a few cozy suites—with hand-plastered walls, shaggy Beni rugs, ‘70s-vibed curved furniture—are available if you want to stay overnight. 

Peacock Pavilions – Our designer friend swears by this peaceful property surrounded by olive groves and live peacocks. “Magnificent interiors curated by the owners with objects trouvé from all over the world. Each room is different. A beautiful pool area in the garden, exceptionally warm and welcoming, with delicious food. An outdoor movie showing classics like Casablanca is a treat. The owners are on property, making the experience even more personal.” 

Mandarin Oriental – A polished resort with plenty of room to breathe, a garden with 100,000 roses, and a Moorish-Arabian design vibe. While it’s hard not to appreciate the sanctuary of a villa with private plunge and a sumptuous spa, and kids will want to spend all day in the football-field sized pool, at night the vibe—particularly the crowd at Ling Ling by Hakkasan—can lean a bit slick. 

Route d’Amizmiz/Agafay (South/Southwest/Atlas Foothills)

Around a 45 minute drive southwest of Marrakech along the Route d’Amizmiz lies the Agafay Desert, known for its moonlike landscape and vast dunes that feel worlds away from the city. It’s set against the backdrop of the Atlas Mountains and has become a hub for desert lodges and design-forward retreats. 

Berber Lodge – The countryside sister to our favorite Riad Mena, this rustically chic 10-room country retreat is the creation of French-Swiss interior designer Romain Michel-Menière, in collaboration with Studio KO. Built on an ancient olive grove using earth from the land itself, the lodge is inspired by traditional Berber architecture, with humble but elevated handcrafted interiors—terracotta tiles, traditional textiles and rattan, antique bride’s chests and pottery. You can also come for lunch in the olive grove—with ingredients from the organic vegetable garden—and use the pool by appointment (adults only). 

Kasbah d’If – Appearing on the desert horizon like a mirage, this kasbah is earthy and elemental—beamed ceilings, limewashed walls, rattan furniture and tile floors. Yolanda stopped in for lunch and loved the rawness of the place: natural tones, carved stone, fringed umbrellas, “pared back in its luxury and simplicity.” Also a great gift shop!

Kasbah Tamadot – Richard Branson discovered the property on a balloon expedition in 1998, and eventually turned it into one of his Virgin Limited hotels. The original Italian owner’s eclectic antiques collection (Indian statues, vintage maps, tapestries) give the main kasbah its character; the tented suites with jacuzzis feel more safari-like. The gardens, pool, tennis court, duck pond and games area mean there’s always something to do, especially for families, but the overall effect can lack the authenticity we tend to seek here. 

Olinto Atlas Mountain Retreat – Franco-Italian furniture collector Prince Fabrizio Raspuli di Poggio Suasa built this adults-only retreat after selling his La Maison Arabe in the city. Suites are housed in private pavilions with richly colored and crafted interiors and heated pools; it is also home to a musical residency program that brings artists to perform and live among the guests.

Selman Marrakech – Just 15 minutes from the medina, this striking, family-owned resort was created by a Moroccan family as a showcase for their beloved Arabian horses. The Moorish-inspired architecture was designed by Jacques Garcia, whose signature drama runs from the sultry cobalt-and-violet lobby to lantern-lit corridors and Chenot spa centered on a stunning indoor pool. Our friends whose daughter is into horses loved the stables and riding program (ponies too!), plus there’s a huge pool and a kids’ club.
